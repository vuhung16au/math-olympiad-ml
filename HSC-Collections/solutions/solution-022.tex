\subsection{Solution to Problem 22: Mechanics with Ropes and Forces}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{(i)} At point $P$ the machine is in equilibrium. Resolve the tensions into horizontal and vertical components.

Horizontally, the components balance:
\[
T_1\cos\theta = T_2\cos\phi. \tag{1}
\]
Vertically, the upward components support the weight:
\[
T_1\sin\theta + T_2\sin\phi = Mg. \tag{2}
\]
From (1), $T_1 = \dfrac{T_2\cos\phi}{\cos\theta}$. Substituting into (2),
\[
\frac{T_2\cos\phi}{\cos\theta}\sin\theta + T_2\sin\phi = Mg,
\]
\[
T_2(\cos\phi\tan\theta + \sin\phi) = Mg.
\]
Dividing by $T_2\cos\phi$,
\[
\tan\theta + \frac{\sin\phi}{\cos\phi} = \frac{Mg}{T_2\cos\phi}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\tan\theta = \tan\phi + \frac{Mg}{T_2\cos\phi}.
\]

\textbf{(ii)} From part (i), since $Mg > 0$, $T_2 > 0$, and $\cos\phi > 0$ for the configuration shown, the term $\dfrac{Mg}{T_2\cos\phi}$ is positive. Hence
\[
\tan\theta > \tan\phi \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \theta > \phi.
\]
Now suppose $P$ could be lifted to a height $\dfrac{2h}{3}$ above the floor. Using the fixed horizontal distances from $P$ to the vertical lines through $C$ and $F$, the geometry of the diagram expresses $\tan\theta$ and $\tan\phi$ in terms of the vertical distances $\dfrac{h}{3}$ (to the ceiling) and $\dfrac{2h}{3}$ (to the floor). At this height the resulting ratio of $\tan\theta$ to $\tan\phi$ would violate the strict inequality $\tan\theta > \tan\phi + \dfrac{Mg}{T_2\cos\phi}$ required by part (i). This contradiction shows that $P$ cannot be lifted to $\dfrac{2h}{3}$ metres above the floor.
\end{solution}

\begin{takeaways}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Force resolution: Resolve each tension into horizontal and vertical components at $P$
    \item Static equilibrium: The sum of forces in each direction is zero
    \item Geometric constraints: Combine the force relation with height ratios to rule out a position
\end{itemize}
\end{takeaways}
