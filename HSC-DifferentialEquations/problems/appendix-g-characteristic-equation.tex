\subsubsection*{Definitions}
Consider a linear homogeneous differential equation with constant coefficients:
\[
a_n y^{(n)}+a_{n-1}y^{(n-1)}+\cdots+a_1y'+a_0y=0.
\]
If we try a solution of the form $y=e^{\lambda x}$, substitution gives
\[
\left(a_n\lambda^n+a_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1\lambda+a_0\right)e^{\lambda x}=0.
\]
Since $e^{\lambda x}\neq 0$, the polynomial factor must vanish.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The polynomial
  \[
  P(\lambda)=a_n\lambda^n+a_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1\lambda+a_0
  \]
  is the \textbf{characteristic polynomial}.
  \item The equation
  \[
  P(\lambda)=0
  \]
  is the \textbf{characteristic equation}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Example 1: First order}
Solve
\[
y'-5y=0.
\]
The characteristic equation is
\[
\lambda-5=0,
\]
so $\lambda=5$ and
\[
y=Ce^{5x}.
\]

\subsubsection*{Example 2: Second order}
Solve
\[
y''+3y'+2y=0.
\]
The characteristic equation is
\[
\lambda^2+3\lambda+2=0=(\lambda+1)(\lambda+2),
\]
so the roots are $-1$ and $-2$. Hence
\[
y=Ae^{-x}+Be^{-2x}.
\]

\subsubsection*{Example 3: Third order}
Solve
\[
y'''=y.
\]
The characteristic equation is
\[
\lambda^3-1=0=(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2+\lambda+1).
\]
This gives one real root $\lambda=1$ and two complex roots
\[
\lambda=-\frac12\pm \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i.
\]
Therefore the general solution is
\[
y=C_1e^x+C_2e^{-x/2}\cos\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}x\right)+C_3e^{-x/2}\sin\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}x\right).
\]

\subsubsection*{Key takeaways}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The method converts a differential-equation problem into an algebra problem about polynomial roots.
  \item Distinct real roots give exponential terms.
  \item Complex roots produce oscillatory sine and cosine terms multiplied by exponentials.
  \item The number and type of roots determine the shape of the general solution.
\end{itemize}
