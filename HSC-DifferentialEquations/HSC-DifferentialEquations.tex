\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Project Overview}

This is a short, exam-focused booklet to help you revise differential equations with solid fundamentals, challenging enrichment problems, and useful appendices for HSC prep.

\subsection{Where This Topic Sits in the NESA Pathway}
This booklet helps you connect differential equations across \textbf{Mathematics Extension~1} and \textbf{Mathematics Extension~2}, especially for \textbf{Mechanics} in Extension~2.

\subsection{Who This Booklet Is For}
This resource is intended for:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Extension~1 students who want a clear, focused differential equations guide,
  \item Extension~2 students who want stronger links between calculus, modelling, and mechanics,
  \item motivated learners who want an early look at how school calculus grows into university maths and engineering,
  \item teachers and tutors who want a concise reference plus enrichment material to stretch stronger students.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{How to Use This Booklet}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Review the fundamentals.
  \item Try problems first without hints, then check and retry. Practice makes progress.
  \item Review yourself using the appendices.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Prerequisite Knowledge}
To use this book well, make sure you:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item have a good understanding of derivatives and anti-derivatives,
  \item are comfortable with basic integrals,
  \item have seen some Year 10--12 physics ideas; if you are rusty, this booklet will refresh what you need as you go.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Extension 2 Mechanics Connections}
These ideas show up a lot in \textbf{Mechanics} and \textbf{Calculus} in Extension~2, and many questions come down to setting up the right differential equation and substitution.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Exponential growth and decay:} $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=ky$ leads to $y=Ae^{kt}$.
  \item \textbf{Resistive motion:} equations such as $m\dfrac{dv}{dt}=mg-kv$ lead to terminal velocity and approach-to-limit behavior.
  \item \textbf{Escape velocity and gravitation:} the substitution $a=v\dfrac{dv}{dx}$ turns force laws into equations in $v$ and $x$.
  \item \textbf{Simple harmonic motion and spring motion:} equations such as $\ddot{x}+n^2x=0$ connect differential equations to oscillation and period.
  \item \textbf{Water tanks and Torricelli-style models:} $\dfrac{dh}{dt}=-k\sqrt{h}$ is a standard separable form.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Choosing the Right Substitution}
Choose the acceleration form that matches the data: use $\dfrac{dv}{dt}$ for time-based equations, $v\dfrac{dv}{dx}$ for velocity-displacement links, and $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{1}{2}v^2\right)$ for energy-style methods. They are equivalent rewrites, so pick the one that makes integration cleaner.

\subsection{Core Syllabus vs Enrichment}
This booklet builds strong \textbf{core HSC differential equations skills} and then pushes you with \textbf{hard enrichment problems}, so you can aim for high marks now and be ready for higher maths later.

\section{Differential Equations}
The core language and notation were introduced in the Fundamentals Review. From here, the focus shifts to using those ideas in practice: reading behavior from slope fields, solving separable forms, and interpreting autonomous models such as logistic growth.

We begin with slope fields, where local gradient information helps us predict solution behavior before full symbolic solving.

\section{Slope Fields}

\subsection{Concept Overview}
A slope field (also called a direction field) is a visual map of the differential equation $y'=F(x,y)$. At each point, you draw a short segment with slope $F(x,y)$, and solution curves follow those local directions.

This lets you read behavior before doing heavy algebra. It is especially useful for spotting equilibrium solutions and predicting long-run trends.

\subsection{Key Ideas}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item If the segment is horizontal, then the slope is zero.
  \item If the segment tilts upward, then the solution increases locally.
  \item If the segment tilts downward, then the solution decreases locally.
  \item A constant solution $y=k$ is an equilibrium if the slope is zero there.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation Notes}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A particular solution must follow the local tangent directions shown by the field.
  \item If nearby segments steer curves toward a horizontal line, that line behaves like a long-run level.
  \item A full answer may require both a sketch and a written description of behavior as $x \to \infty$ or $x \to -\infty$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item For $yy'+x=0$, the line $y=0$ is a singular row where the slope formula $y'=-x/y$ is undefined.
  \item For $y'=-x-y$, the line $y=-x$ is an isocline of zero slope.
  \item For $y'=xy$, both axes are zero-slope lines, and the sign of $xy$ controls whether the local segments tilt up or down.
\end{itemize}

\section{Separable Differential Equations}

\subsection{Concept Overview}
Many HSC differential equations are separable, which means you can move the $y$ terms and $x$ terms to opposite sides before integrating. This is one of the main algebraic techniques you need to master.

\subsection{Canonical Forms}
\[
\frac{dy}{dx}=f(x),
\qquad
\frac{dy}{dx}=g(y),
\qquad
\frac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y).
\]

For a separable equation, we aim for a structure like
\[
A(y)\,dy = B(x)\,dx.
\]

\subsection{Interpretation Notes}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The form $y'=f(x)$ means the slope depends only on $x$.
  \item The form $y'=g(y)$ means the slope depends only on the current value of $y$.
  \item The form $y'=f(x)g(y)$ mixes both influences and is often the richest case.
\end{itemize}

% \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]

%   \item Dropping absolute values when integrating logarithmic forms.
%   \item Forgetting to substitute the initial condition back into the integrated solution.
% \end{itemize}

\begin{enrichmentbox}
At university level, students later meet equations that are not separable and need different methods. Learning to recognize separable structure now builds the pattern-recognition needed for those later techniques.
\end{enrichmentbox}

\section{Autonomous Equations, Equilibria, and the Logistic Model}

\subsection{Concept Overview}
An autonomous differential equation has the form $y'=g(y)$, so the slope depends only on the current value of $y$. These equations often make equilibrium values and long-run behavior easier to see.

This section focuses on equilibrium and stability analysis for autonomous equations. The logistic equation is a key worked example because it combines growth with a limiting effect: a quantity grows when small, then slows as it approaches a carrying capacity.

\subsection{Equilibria and Stability (Phase-Line Thinking)}
For an autonomous equation $y'=g(y)$, equilibrium values occur where $g(y)=0$. To classify behavior, check the sign of $g(y)$ in intervals between equilibrium values:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item if $g(y)>0$, solutions increase;
  \item if $g(y)<0$, solutions decrease;
  \item if nearby arrows point toward an equilibrium, it is stable;
  \item if nearby arrows point away, it is unstable.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Key Forms}
\[
\frac{dy}{dx}=g(y)
\]

\[
\frac{dy}{dx}=ky(M-y)
\qquad \text{or} \qquad
\frac{dy}{dx}=ry\left(1-\frac{y}{M}\right).
\]

\subsection{Interpretation Notes}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item In the logistic model, $y=0$ and $y=M$ are equilibrium values.
  \item If $0<y<M$, growth is positive.
  \item If $y>M$, the rate is negative and the model pushes back toward $M$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Other Common Autonomous Models}
The logistic equation is one important model, but not the only one. Other standard autonomous forms include:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Exponential growth/decay: $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=ky$.
  \item Newton cooling/approach-to-ambient: $\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a)$.
  \item Logistic with harvesting: $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=ry\left(1-\dfrac{y}{M}\right)-h$.
\end{itemize}
These can all be analysed with the same equilibrium-and-sign method used for logistic growth.

\begin{enrichmentbox}
Stable/unstable equilibrium language and phase-line analysis become very important in engineering, dynamical systems, and mathematical biology. The logistic equation is often a student's first encounter with these ideas, but the same framework extends to many other models.
\end{enrichmentbox}

\section{Conclusion}
Differential equations are one of the clearest examples of how calculus becomes a modelling language. The topic asks students to do more than differentiate and integrate: it asks them to connect rate, graph, formula, and interpretation in a single chain of reasoning. That combination is exactly why the topic is so useful later in Extension~2, mechanics, engineering, and university mathematics.

From what I have seen in HSC Math Extension 1 exams in recent years, the most asked questions on this topic are: (i) understanding slope fields, (ii) solving separable equations, and (iii) interpreting logistic growth models with difficulty levels ranging from basic to medium in this booklet. The more advanced problems in this booklet are designed to stretch your understanding and problem-solving skills, and they may help you prepare for Mechanics problems in Extension~2 or university courses.

As you work through this booklet, aim to build two habits together: solve carefully, and explain what the solution means. Strong HSC answers usually do both. Once the problem bank is filled in, this booklet will become a natural place to practice that full cycle repeatedly.
