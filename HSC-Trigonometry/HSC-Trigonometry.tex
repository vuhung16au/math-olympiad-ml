\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Project Overview}
This booklet is a growing collection of trigonometry problems for NSW HSC Mathematics Extension~1 and Extension~2 students. It covers the full trigonometry strand across both syllabuses, including right-angled triangles, trigonometric functions of a general angle, trigonometric identities and equations, the sine and cosine rules, inverse trigonometric functions, compound and double-angle formulae, the $t$-formulae, and products-to-sums conversions. The problems range from straightforward skill-drill questions to multi-step challenge problems suitable for exam preparation and beyond.

\subsection{Target Audience}
This resource is written for students who want focused, exam-oriented practice with trigonometry in a clear, classroom-friendly format. Solutions are intentionally concise but each one highlights the key idea behind the answer. Students who work systematically through Parts~1 and~2 should gain both fluency and a deeper conceptual understanding of the trigonometry topics tested in Extension~1 and Extension~2.

\subsection{How to Use This Booklet}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Read the core formulas and theory summaries in this introduction first.
  \item Attempt each problem before reading the solution.
  \item Pay attention to the \emph{type} of question: is it an identity, an equation, a geometry application, or an inverse trigonometry question?
  \item Look for structure: symmetry, compound-angle patterns, Pythagorean identities, or opportunities to substitute $t = \tan(\theta/2)$.
  \item Use the remarks and takeaways to build pattern recognition for future HSC questions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Extension 1 Knowledge Needed}
Most problems in this booklet can be solved using standard HSC Mathematics Extension~1 and Extension~2 knowledge. The key background material is:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Right-angled trigonometry:} definitions of $\sin\theta$, $\cos\theta$, $\tan\theta$ as ratios of opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse; exact values for $30^\circ$, $45^\circ$, $60^\circ$.
  \item \textbf{ASTC rule (quadrant signs):} All, Sine, Tangent, Cosine are positive in quadrants~I, II, III, IV respectively.
  \item \textbf{Trigonometric functions of a general angle:} converting angles to the related acute angle and applying the correct sign.
  \item \textbf{Fundamental identity:} $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ and its derived forms $1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$ and $1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta$.
  \item \textbf{Double and symmetry identities:} supplementary ($\pi - \theta$), complementary ($\pi/2 - \theta$), and reflex ($2\pi - \theta$) angle reductions.
  \item \textbf{The sine rule and cosine rule:} applied to general (non-right-angled) triangles, including the ambiguous case.
  \item \textbf{Area formula:} $\text{Area} = \tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C$.
  \item \textbf{Inverse trigonometric functions:} restricted domains for $\arcsin$, $\arccos$, $\arctan$ and graphical properties.
  \item \textbf{Compound-angle identities:} $\sin(\alpha \pm \beta)$, $\cos(\alpha \pm \beta)$, $\tan(\alpha \pm \beta)$.
  \item \textbf{Double-angle formulae:} $\sin 2\theta$, $\cos 2\theta$ (all three forms), $\tan 2\theta$.
  \item \textbf{The $t$-formulae:} expressing $\sin\theta$, $\cos\theta$, $\tan\theta$ in terms of $t = \tan(\theta/2)$.
  \item \textbf{Products-to-sums and sums-to-products:} converting between products and sums of trigonometric functions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Core Formulas and Ideas}

\textbf{Exact Values.}
\[
\begin{array}{c|ccc}
\theta & \sin\theta & \cos\theta & \tan\theta \\[2pt]\hline\\[-8pt]
0^\circ & 0 & 1 & 0 \\[4pt]
30^\circ & \dfrac{1}{2} & \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \\[10pt]
45^\circ & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 1 \\[10pt]
60^\circ & \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \dfrac{1}{2} & \sqrt{3} \\[10pt]
90^\circ & 1 & 0 & \text{undefined}
\end{array}
\]

\textbf{Pythagorean Identities.}
\[
\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1, \qquad
1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta, \qquad
1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta.
\]

\textbf{Sine Rule.}
\[
\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} = 2R,
\]
where $R$ is the circumradius of the triangle.

\textbf{Cosine Rule.}
\[
c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C, \qquad
\cos C = \frac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2ab}.
\]

\textbf{Area Formula.}
\[
\text{Area} = \tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C.
\]

\subsection{Angle Reduction Identities}

For all angles $\theta$:
\[
\sin(\pi - \theta) = \sin\theta, \qquad \cos(\pi-\theta) = -\cos\theta, \qquad \tan(\pi-\theta) = -\tan\theta.
\]
\[
\sin(\pi + \theta) = -\sin\theta, \qquad \cos(\pi+\theta) = -\cos\theta, \qquad \tan(\pi+\theta) = \tan\theta.
\]
\[
\sin(2\pi - \theta) = -\sin\theta, \qquad \cos(2\pi - \theta) = \cos\theta.
\]
\[
\sin\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right) = \cos\theta, \qquad \cos\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right) = \sin\theta.
\]

\subsection{Compound Angle Identities}
\[
\sin(\alpha \pm \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta \pm \cos\alpha\sin\beta.
\]
\[
\cos(\alpha \pm \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta \mp \sin\alpha\sin\beta.
\]
\[
\tan(\alpha \pm \beta) = \frac{\tan\alpha \pm \tan\beta}{1 \mp \tan\alpha\tan\beta}.
\]

\subsection{Double-Angle Formulae}
\[
\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta.
\]
\[
\cos 2\theta = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1.
\]
\[
\tan 2\theta = \frac{2\tan\theta}{1 - \tan^2\theta}.
\]

The power-reduction forms (useful for integration in Extension~2):
\[
\sin^2\theta = \frac{1-\cos 2\theta}{2}, \qquad \cos^2\theta = \frac{1+\cos 2\theta}{2}.
\]

\subsection{The $t$-Formulae}

Let $t = \tan\!\left(\dfrac{\theta}{2}\right)$. Then:
\[
\sin\theta = \frac{2t}{1+t^2}, \qquad
\cos\theta = \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}, \qquad
\tan\theta = \frac{2t}{1-t^2}.
\]
These substitutions convert trigonometric equations into polynomial equations in $t$, a powerful technique for solving equations of the form $a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta = c$.

\begin{remark}
The $t$-substitution introduces the extraneous restriction $\theta \neq \pi + 2k\pi$ (where $\tan(\theta/2)$ is undefined). Always check that solutions from the $t$-substitution are valid in the original equation, and consider separately whether $\theta = \pi$ is a solution.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Products-to-Sums and Sums-to-Products}

\textbf{Products to sums:}
\[
\sin\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\sin(\alpha+\beta)+\sin(\alpha-\beta)].
\]
\[
\cos\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(\alpha-\beta)+\cos(\alpha+\beta)].
\]
\[
\sin\alpha\sin\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(\alpha-\beta)-\cos(\alpha+\beta)].
\]

\textbf{Sums to products:}
\[
\sin A + \sin B = 2\sin\!\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\cos\!\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right).
\]
\[
\sin A - \sin B = 2\cos\!\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\sin\!\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right).
\]
\[
\cos A + \cos B = 2\cos\!\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\cos\!\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right).
\]
\[
\cos A - \cos B = -2\sin\!\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\sin\!\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right).
\]

\subsection{Inverse Trigonometric Functions}

The three principal inverse functions and their restricted domains are:
\[
\arcsin : [-1,1] \to \left[-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right], \qquad
\arccos : [-1,1] \to [0, \pi], \qquad
\arctan : \mathbb{R} \to \left(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right).
\]

\textbf{Key identities:}
\[
\arcsin(-x) = -\arcsin x, \qquad \arccos(-x) = \pi - \arccos x, \qquad \arctan(-x) = -\arctan x.
\]
\[
\arcsin x + \arccos x = \frac{\pi}{2} \quad \text{for } x \in [-1,1].
\]

\subsection{Auxiliary Angle Form}

Any expression of the form $a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta$ can be written as a single sinusoid:
\[
a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta = R\sin(\theta + \varphi),
\]
where
\[
R = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}, \qquad \tan\varphi = \frac{b}{a}.
\]
Similarly, $a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta = R\cos(\theta - \psi)$ where $\tan\psi = a/b$.

This form is extremely useful for finding the maximum and minimum values of weighted combinations of sine and cosine, and for solving equations of the form $a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta = c$.

\subsection{Three-Dimensional Trigonometry}

Many HSC problems extend two-dimensional trigonometry to three-dimensional figures (pyramids, prisms, slanted poles, etc.). A reliable method is:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Draw a clear diagram and label all known lengths and angles.
  \item Identify the right-angled triangles embedded in the figure.
  \item Work systematically from known information, using the sine rule or cosine rule where required.
  \item Use the angle of elevation or depression carefully: it is always measured from the horizontal.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Problem-Solving Habits for This Booklet}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Always write down the domain of $\theta$ before solving a trigonometric equation.
  \item Check whether the identity or formula requires degrees or radians.
  \item For the sine rule, always check the ambiguous case when the given angle is acute.
  \item In identity proofs, start from the more complex side and aim to simplify.
  \item For inverse trigonometric function questions, sketch the graph to confirm the range.
  \item In $t$-substitution problems, remember to check $\theta = \pi$ separately.
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}
The most common trigonometry mistake is ignoring the full solution set when solving equations. For example, $\sin\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}$ has infinitely many solutions; always write the general solution and then restrict to the given domain.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why trigonometry matters beyond the HSC]
Trigonometric functions appear throughout mathematics, physics, engineering, and data science. Fourier analysis decomposes any periodic signal -- sound, images, electrical waveforms -- into sums of sines and cosines. The $t$-substitution and product-to-sum identities that appear in HSC Extension~2 are the same tools used in signal processing, control theory, and quantum mechanics.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

Trigonometry rewards careful attention to detail as much as formula recall. Many HSC questions become far more tractable once you identify the correct strategy: choosing the right form of $\cos 2\theta$, recognising a products-to-sums pattern, or knowing instinctively when the $t$-substitution will linearise an equation. As this booklet grows, it will serve as a compact reference for the most important trigonometric patterns students encounter in Extension~1 and Extension~2 examinations and beyond.
